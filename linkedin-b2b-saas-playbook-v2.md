# LinkedIn Organic Content Strategy Playbook for B2B SaaS

**Topic:** LinkedIn Organic Content Strategy for B2B SaaS  
**Research Base:** 10 Expert Practitioners  

---

## Preface

This is not a listicle. It's a working operator's guide built from synthesizing 10 of the most credible voices in LinkedIn-driven B2B SaaS growth. I've read their posts, watched their talks, stress-tested their frameworks against real execution, and made deliberate choices about what to keep, what to discard, and where I think they're wrong.

The goal: a playbook you can actually run.

## Section 1: Core Recommendations (With Sources)

### 1.1 — Write for One Person, Not One Audience

Stop writing for "B2B decision-makers." Write for a specific human in a specific situation. Picture your ideal reader having a bad Tuesday — what are they thinking about? Write that.

Before you write any post, answer this: *Who is the one person this is for, and what are they dealing with right now?* A VP of Sales at a 50-person SaaS company worrying about missed quota. A founder who just hired their first marketing hire and doesn't know what to ask of them. A CS lead drowning in onboarding tickets. Name the person. Then write to them.

- **Why it works:** Specificity creates recognition. When someone reads a post and thinks "this is exactly me," they comment, share, and remember you. Generic writing slides past everyone. Specific writing stops the right person cold.
- **Source:** Justin Welsh, LinkedIn post on "writing for one reader vs. one audience" — a recurring theme in his LinkedIn Operating System course and public posts (2023–2024), justinwelsh.me

---

### 1.2 — Lead Every Post With a Hook That Creates a Gap

Your first line must do one thing: make the next line unignorable. LinkedIn collapses posts after 2–3 lines. If the hook doesn't earn the scroll, nothing below it matters.

There are three hook types that consistently work in B2B SaaS:

**Type 1 — The Contradiction:** Challenges what your audience assumes to be true.
> *"We cut our LinkedIn posting frequency in half. Pipeline from content doubled."*

**Type 2 — The Specific Curiosity:** Names a specific number, outcome, or person — then withholds the explanation.
> *"One post generated 3 enterprise demo requests in 48 hours. Here's exactly what it said."*

**Type 3 — The Direct Utility:** Makes a specific, credible promise of value in the first line.
> *"The 4-line email sequence our AEs use to re-engage gone-dark prospects. Steal it."*

Avoid: "I want to talk about something important today." Avoid: "Unpopular opinion:" (overused and now invisible). Avoid starting with "I" — LinkedIn buries posts that open with "I" slightly more than posts that don't.

- **Why it works:** The algorithm amplifies posts with high "See more" click-through. A weak hook = invisible post, no matter how good the content underneath.
- **Source:** Devin Reed, *The Reeder* newsletter — specifically his breakdowns of Gong's highest-performing LinkedIn posts and his framework for "the first line is everything" (2024), thereeder.co

---

### 1.3 — Post 3–5x Per Week. Not Daily. Not Whenever.

3–5 times a week is the sweet spot. Daily posting burns your credibility reservoir and trains your audience to expect quantity over quality. Monthly posting means the algorithm forgets you exist.

**Suggested weekly breakdown for a B2B SaaS founder or CMO:**

| Day | Post Type | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | POV / Opinion post | Set the frame for the week. Strong opinion on a market truth. |
| Wednesday | Tactical / How-to post | Deliver specific value. What you've learned or built. |
| Friday | Story / Case post | A deal story, customer win, or internal lesson with a clear takeaway. |

Optional 4th post (Tuesday or Thursday): Engagement post — a question, a poll, or a short observation. Keep it simple. Don't overthink it.

This structure prevents the most common failure mode: posting the same type of content five days in a row and wondering why engagement drops by Thursday.

- **Why it works:** LinkedIn's algorithm rewards recency AND consistency. 3–5x/week keeps you active in feeds without diluting content quality.
- **Source:** Chris Walker, *Demand Gen Live* podcast — episode on "how often B2B founders should post on LinkedIn" and his LinkedIn posts on content cadence vs. content quality (2024–2025), chriswalker.fm

---

### 1.4 — Zero-Click Content: Give Full Value Inside the Post

Stop baiting people to your blog. Give the full insight in the post itself. If someone can learn nothing without clicking, they'll scroll past.

What zero-click content looks like in practice: Instead of "I wrote about the 3 biggest mistakes B2B SaaS companies make on LinkedIn — link in comments," you write the 3 mistakes, explain each one in 2–3 lines, and end with a clean takeaway. The whole post fits in the LinkedIn window. No link in the body.

If you want to drive to a newsletter or resource, put the link in the *first comment* after posting — not in the post body. This is the standard workaround and still works.

- **Why it works:** LinkedIn suppresses posts with external links in the main body. More importantly, audiences have trained themselves to ignore "click here for more" posts. Zero-click content builds trust faster because it respects the reader's time and delivers on the implicit promise of the hook.
- **Source:** Amanda Natividad, Zero-Click Content Framework — coined and expanded in her SparkToro writing and LinkedIn posts on "how to create content that works without requiring a click" (2023–2024), sparktoro.com/blog

---

### 1.5 — Repurpose Systematically, Not Randomly

One piece of original insight should produce multiple assets — but only if you have a system. Random repurposing is just reposting with a different format. Systematic repurposing extracts every angle from one core idea.

**The 1-to-4 Repurposing Map:**

Start with one insight (e.g., "BOFU content generates 3x more pipeline than TOFU content for our segment"):

1. **LinkedIn text post** — The insight as a punchy, zero-click narrative post (Mon or Wed)
2. **LinkedIn carousel** — The same insight broken into 6–8 slides with a visual framework
3. **Short video script** — A 60–90 second talking-head script: problem, insight, proof, action
4. **Newsletter section** — Expanded version with more context, data, and nuance for subscribers

Do this for your 3 best insights per month. That's 12 assets from 3 ideas — not 12 pieces of content from 12 different ideas that never go anywhere.

- **Why it works:** Distribution beats creation every time. One insight properly distributed beats 10 thrown into the void. The insight also compounds — each format reaches a different slice of your audience.
- **Source:** Devin Reed, content repurposing system built while leading content at Gong — documented in LinkedIn posts on "how Gong turned one idea into 10 pieces of content" (2024), thereeder.co

---

### 1.6 — Build a Point of View, Not a Content Calendar

Your LinkedIn presence should have a clear intellectual thesis — 2–3 ideas you believe are true that most people in your space don't fully accept. Own those ideas relentlessly. Every post should tie back to your core POV.

**How to define your POV in 30 minutes:**

Answer these three questions in writing:
1. What do most people in my market believe that I think is wrong or incomplete?
2. What has my experience shown me that most people haven't seen yet?
3. If someone followed me for 6 months, what should they believe that they don't believe today?

Your answers are your POV pillars. Write them down as single, declarative sentences:
- *"Demand gen for B2B SaaS is broken because it optimizes for MQLs, not revenue."*
- *"PLG and sales-led are not opposing strategies — companies that separate them leave money on the table."*

Every post you write should either state, prove, challenge, or expand one of these pillars.

- **Why it works:** People follow people with strong opinions. Generic insight gives readers no reason to follow you specifically over the 50 other SaaS marketers posting similar content.
- **Source:** Dave Gerhardt, Exit Five podcast and community — specifically his repeated argument that "B2B marketers need a point of view, not a posting schedule" and his LinkedIn posts on building a personal brand through intellectual conviction (2024), exitfive.com

---

### 1.7 — BOFU Content Converts. Most People Skip It.

Bottom-of-funnel content on LinkedIn means content that speaks to people who are close to a purchase decision — not people who are just discovering your category. Most LinkedIn content is TOFU awareness fluff. BOFU content is undersupplied.

**Four BOFU formats that work:**

1. **Comparison post:** "How we think about [Your Product] vs. [Competitor/Alternative] — and when we'd tell you NOT to buy us."
2. **Decision walkthrough:** "Why one of our customers switched from [incumbent] to us — and what they wish they'd known before the eval."
3. **Objection neutralizer:** Story-format post that addresses the #1 reason deals don't close, without naming it as an objection (see Section 4).
4. **Product-in-action post:** Show a specific workflow, outcome, or result inside your product. Not a demo — a moment of "oh, that's useful."

The test for BOFU: Would a buyer actively evaluating your category find this useful while doing their research? If yes, publish it.

- **Why it works:** BOFU content is undersupplied because most content teams are scared to talk about product — they think it's too "salesy." It isn't. It's what the right buyers are actually looking for.
- **Source:** Lashay Lewis, *Authority Plug* — her LinkedIn posts and newsletter on "why BOFU content is the most underrated B2B SaaS LinkedIn strategy" and her framework for content that "converts, not just attracts" (2024–2025), authorityplug.com

---

### 1.8 — Write With Voice, Not Just Information

Removing your writing's personality doesn't make it more professional. It makes it forgettable. Good B2B writing sounds like a real person who actually knows what they're talking about.

Three ways to inject voice without sacrificing clarity:
- **Use the word "actually."** It signals a genuine belief, not a polished talking point. *"Most content strategies actually fail before the first post goes out."*
- **Name what you won't do.** Voice includes conviction about what you reject. *"We don't do weekly 'tips' posts. Here's what we do instead."*
- **Write the sentence you'd say out loud.** Read your draft aloud. If you'd never say it in a meeting, rewrite it.

- **Why it works:** Voice is differentiation. There are thousands of posts about "5 growth hacks." There's only one post written in your voice. Buyers buy from people they trust, and trust comes faster from a voice that feels human than from one that sounds like a press release.
- **Source:** Ann Handley, *Everybody Writes* (2nd ed., 2022) — specifically Chapter 4 on "writing with honest voice" and her MarketingProfs newsletter on why B2B writing is broken, annhandley.com

---

### 1.9 — Use Content for Sales Enablement, Not Just Marketing

Your LinkedIn posts should feed your sales team. When a rep sends a prospect a post from your founder or CMO that speaks directly to a pain point, the conversation changes — and the rep didn't have to write a word.

**How to operationalize this:**
1. Every week, tag 1–2 posts as "sales-ready" in your Slack or CRM.
2. Brief the sales team on what the post addresses and *when to send it* (e.g., "send this to any prospect who said onboarding complexity is a concern").
3. Build a simple Notion or Google Doc with posts organized by objection/use case. AEs self-serve.

This turns your content function into a sales asset, not just a brand asset. The measurement shifts from impressions to pipeline influenced.

- **Why it works:** It collapses trust-building timelines. The prospect has already consumed your company's thinking before the call. Objections come pre-handled. The rep walks in as a peer, not a salesperson.
- **Source:** Emily Kramer, *MKT1 Newsletter* — her piece on "how great B2B marketing teams align content with the sales funnel" and her framework for content-to-sales handoff (2024), mkt1.co

---

### 1.10 — Personal Brand Outperforms Company Page, Always

Your company LinkedIn page has reach. Your personal brand has trust. In B2B SaaS, trust closes deals. Invest in people-led content — founders, execs, subject-matter experts — not just the company brand page.

Organic reach from a personal profile regularly outperforms a company page by 5–10x on LinkedIn. That's not a growth hack — it's platform architecture. LinkedIn was built for people, not brands.

- **Why it works:** People follow people. LinkedIn's algorithm actively favors personal profiles over company pages in organic reach. And in B2B, the founder or CMO's credibility often transfers directly to the product.
- **Source:** Lara Acosta, LinkedIn posts on "why personal brands beat company pages for B2B reach" — a consistent theme across her 2024–2025 content on LinkedIn strategy, laraacosta.com (308K+ followers)

---

### 1.11 — PLG Companies Need Different Content Than Sales-Led

If your product has a free tier or self-serve onboarding, your LinkedIn content should drive product discovery, not demo requests. Content that shows the product in action, use cases, and quick wins matters more than demand gen.

**The content shift for PLG:**

| Sales-Led | PLG |
|---|---|
| "Book a demo to see how X works" | "Here's how one team set up X in 20 minutes" |
| ROI frameworks and business case content | "What you can do with your first 30 days free" |
| Comparison vs. competitors | "When to start with the free tier vs. paid" |
| Case studies with logos | User-generated quick-win stories |

- **Why it works:** PLG audiences self-qualify. Content that shows "here's what you can do with this" converts better than "book a call" for self-serve products. The goal is product activation, not pipeline.
- **Source:** Elena Verna, LinkedIn posts on "why PLG and sales-led require fundamentally different content strategies" — she made this argument repeatedly based on her experience at Dropbox, Miro, and SurveyMonkey (2024), elenaverna.com

## The LinkedIn Post Structure Framework

Use this as a starting template for any post. It's not a rigid formula — it's a thinking tool.

```
1. HOOK (1–2 lines)
   Create a gap. Make the reader need to know what comes next.
   → Contradiction, specific curiosity, or direct utility.

2. INSIGHT (3–5 lines)
   The core idea. What do you actually believe or know?
   → State it clearly. Don't bury it. Don't over-qualify it.

3. EXAMPLE (2–4 lines)
   Proof of the insight. A real story, a real number, a real scenario.
   → "Here's what this looked like in practice..."
   → Specificity is credibility. Vague examples kill good insights.

4. TAKEAWAY (1–2 lines)
   What should the reader do, believe, or reconsider?
   → Make it actionable or thought-provoking — never both at once.
   → End with a line that stays with them. Not a question. A statement.
```

**Example applied:**

> *Hook:* We ran the same LinkedIn experiment twice. Different results both times. Here's what actually changed.
>
> *Insight:* Posting frequency matters less than posting timing. Most SaaS operators obsess over "how often" when the real variable is "to whom, when."
>
> *Example:* We moved posting from 9am to 7:45am on Tuesday/Thursday. Same content. Reach increased 34% over 6 weeks. The feed is less crowded before business hours hit.
>
> *Takeaway:* Don't optimize the content until you've optimized the timing. Small variables compound faster than people expect.

## Section 2: Where Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1: Consistency vs. Quality — Which Matters More?

**Justin Welsh says:** Post consistently, even if the content isn't perfect. Volume teaches you faster than perfection. He built to 650K+ followers with 2+ years of daily/near-daily posting.

**Ann Handley says:** Quality and voice matter more than volume. Don't post unless you have something worth saying. A single great piece outlasts 30 mediocre ones.

**Why they differ:** Justin is building a personal brand product (courses, newsletter) where follower count is directly monetizable. Ann is building long-term authority in a more traditional marketing context. Volume makes sense when your business model rewards audience size. Quality-first makes sense when your business is reputation-driven.

**My call: Ann is right for B2B SaaS operators.** If you're a founder, CMO, or growth leader posting to build pipeline — not to sell a personal brand course — quality wins every time. Posting thin content 5x/week trains your audience to ignore you. One genuinely useful post per week that makes someone stop and think is worth more than daily noise. Justin's playbook works for Justin. It doesn't automatically port to a B2B SaaS context.

---

### Disagreement 2: Zero-Click vs. Drive Traffic to Long-Form

**Amanda Natividad says:** Keep value inside the post. Zero-click content builds trust, avoids link suppression, and respects audience time. Your newsletter/blog captures the engaged minority anyway.

**Dave Gerhardt says:** Use LinkedIn as the top of the funnel. Create curiosity, then drive to long-form — podcast, newsletter, article — where you can go deeper and capture emails.

**Why they differ:** Amanda is optimizing for LinkedIn-native reach and trust. Dave is optimizing for owned audience growth (Exit Five's model is community + email). Both are right depending on your goal, but they're solving different problems.

**My call: Amanda wins for content that closes pipeline. Dave wins for audience-building campaigns.** If your immediate goal is pipeline from LinkedIn, go zero-click — keep the decision-maker on platform, give them the full insight, earn their trust. If you're in a longer campaign to build a subscriber base or community, Dave's approach is more systematic. For most B2B SaaS companies, pipeline > subscriber count, so default to zero-click.

---

### Disagreement 3: Founder-Led Content vs. Team-Led Content

**Chris Walker says:** Founder-led content is powerful, but the real leverage is when a whole team posts — sales reps, SDRs, customer success leads. Company-wide LinkedIn presence compounds in a way no single founder can match.

**Lara Acosta says:** One person with a strong personal brand and a consistent voice beats a fragmented team where everyone posts differently. Coherence matters more than volume.

**Why they differ:** Chris is a CEO and GTM consultant thinking at company scale. Lara is a personal brand builder. Chris is right that distribution expands when more people post, but wrong to underestimate the coordination cost and quality dilution that comes with team-wide posting without tight guidelines.

**My call: Start with one strong voice, then expand.** Pick the person at your company who can actually write and has genuine opinions — usually the founder or CMO. Get their content right first. Once you have a working formula and a POV that people recognize, then teach it to the team. Flipping this order gets you a flood of forgettable posts that confuse the audience about what your company actually believes.

## Section 3: What I Rejected and Why

### Rejected Idea 1: "Post Every Single Day, No Matter What"

**Suggested by:** Justin Welsh (implicitly, through his own behavior and advice on volume)

**Why it sounds good:** Volume creates habit. Daily posting builds algorithmic momentum. More at-bats = more learning.

**Why I rejected it:** This advice was built for a specific context — a solopreneur whose business model is the content itself. Justin's revenue comes from followers buying his courses. Frequency is fuel for that flywheel. For a B2B SaaS founder or marketer, daily posting without genuine daily insight doesn't build credibility — it erodes it. Your CTO, VP Sales, or target buyer doesn't want a thought for the day. They want sharp, specific, useful content. Posting daily to hit a number is how you become a person your audience learned to ignore.

---

### Rejected Idea 2: "Engage on 20–30 Posts Per Day to Grow Faster"

**Suggested by:** Lara Acosta (in multiple posts about LinkedIn growth tactics, 2024)

**Why it sounds good:** Comments put your name in front of large audiences. Strategic commenting under big creators can drive real follower growth. The math works on paper.

**Why I rejected it:** This is a personal brand growth hack, not a B2B pipeline strategy. A VP of Marketing or a SaaS founder commenting 25 times a day on other people's posts looks like it has no better use of time. Worse, most "strategic commenting" descends into generic affirmation ("great point!") that actively damages your perceived credibility. For B2B SaaS, your authority comes from original insight, not from being the loudest commenter in a thread. This tactic is optimized for follower count — a vanity metric for most SaaS companies.

## Section 4: My Original Ideas

### Original Idea 1: The "Objection Post" Series

**The idea:** Map your top 5–7 sales objections, then write one LinkedIn post per objection that neutralizes it — without ever mentioning it's addressing an objection. Write it as a genuine insight or story.

Example: If the top sales objection is "we don't have the bandwidth to implement this," write a post called "How one team ran our entire integration in 4 hours without involving IT" — then let the story do the work.

**Why it could work:** Your sales team already knows what buyers push back on. That's a content brief sitting unused. A prospect who reads this post before a discovery call arrives pre-handled. The objection has already been confronted and answered in their mind. This directly compresses sales cycles.

**When to use it:** When you have a predictable sales process with repeating objections. Minimum 3–5 closed deals to identify real objection patterns.

**Risk/downside:** If the posts are too obviously "objection laundering," sophisticated buyers will notice and find it manipulative. The writing has to be genuinely useful and story-led, not a thinly veiled FAQ.

---

### Original Idea 2: The "Deal Anatomy" Post Format

**The idea:** Write regular posts that walk through a real deal — what the buyer's situation was, what they were evaluating, why they chose you, what almost stopped them. Anonymize where needed. This is deal teardown content.

**Why it could work:** Most LinkedIn content tells buyers *what* to think. Deal anatomy posts show buyers *how deals like theirs actually get decided*. It's social proof, sales enablement, and insight content all in one. No expert in this space is doing this systematically.

**When to use it:** When you have 5+ closed deals with clear narratives. Best suited for mid-market or enterprise SaaS where deal complexity is high.

**Risk/downside:** Requires buy-in from the sales team to share real deal intelligence. Confidentiality is a genuine concern — one customer who recognizes themselves and feels exposed could damage a relationship.

## Section 5: Weaknesses of This Playbook

I'd be doing you a disservice if I didn't say this plainly.

**1. Most research is self-reported.**
Every expert in this list is telling you what worked for them, in their context, in their time. Justin Welsh's LinkedIn results were built when the platform was less saturated. Chris Walker's demand gen model was validated at Refine Labs in a specific market environment (2020–2023). The algorithm has changed. The audience has grown. Past results are real but not portable by default.

**2. The playbook is biased toward content-led, not code-led growth.**
If your ICP is technical (CTOs, engineers, DevOps leads), most of these experts' audiences don't overlap with yours. Their hooks, formats, and posting cadences work for marketing, sales, and founder audiences. You may need to adapt significantly for developer-focused SaaS.

**3. There are no hard attribution numbers.**
None of this research is backed by a controlled test showing "X LinkedIn strategy produced Y pipeline." It's practitioner experience, which is the best data available — but it's still qualitative. You cannot run this playbook and expect linear results. Content attribution is messy and long-cycle.

**4. The "zero-click" vs. "drive traffic" debate is unresolved here.**
I made a judgment call (zero-click wins for pipeline), but the honest answer is this needs to be tested in your specific context. Your audience, your ICP, your product — all of these change the math.

**5. No ICP-specific calibration.**
This playbook is written for B2B SaaS generically. Whether you're selling to SMBs at $50/month or enterprise at $100K/year dramatically changes what content converts. This playbook gives you the framework. You need to do the ICP calibration yourself.

## Section 6: Who I Would NOT Recommend Following Blindly

### Do Not Follow Justin Welsh as a Template for B2B SaaS Companies

**What he recommends:** Daily posting, personal brand as product, high-volume consistency, frameworks packaged for broad audiences.

**Why it works sometimes:** It absolutely works for solopreneurs building audience-first businesses. His system is internally consistent and produces real results — for him. If you are building a one-person content business, his playbook is worth studying.

**Why you should not copy it:** Justin is not selling SaaS. His business *is* his audience. When you apply his volume and format templates to a B2B SaaS context, you end up with posts that feel like personal development content — generic, broadly applicable, and entirely forgettable to a B2B buyer. His hooks ("I failed for 2 years and then did X") work because they're relatable to aspiring solopreneurs. They don't resonate with a VP of RevOps evaluating software. His success also predates the current level of LinkedIn saturation with "thought leaders." Replicating his approach in 2026 produces very different results than it did in 2022.

---

### Do Not Follow Lara Acosta's Growth Tactics Without Heavy Filtering

**What she recommends:** High-frequency posting, aggressive commenting for reach, strong visual hooks, personal vulnerability-driven content.

**Why it works sometimes:** For personal brand building and audience growth metrics, her system works. She has 308K+ followers and strong post engagement. Her hooks are genuinely sharp and worth studying.

**Why you should not copy it:** Her content model is optimized for creator metrics — follower count, likes, virality. Most of her tactics (comment baiting, heavy personal narrative, generic inspiration angles) do not translate to pipeline in B2B SaaS. A founder who posts "I almost quit 3 times before I hit $1M" gets engagement from aspiring entrepreneurs — not from SaaS buyers evaluating a product. The audience she builds for you is probably not the audience that converts for you. Use her for hook structure and formatting ideas. Do not use her as a strategic model.

## Section 7: 7-Day Execution SOP

This section exists because insight without execution is just reading. If you've made it this far, here's exactly how you go from "I understand this playbook" to "I am running this playbook."

This is a realistic 7-day sprint designed for a founder, CMO, or senior marketer who can spend 60–90 minutes per day. It's not a shortcut. It's the minimum viable start.

---

### Day 1 — Profile Audit + POV Definition (90 min)

**Goal:** Establish your foundation before writing a single post.

**Morning (45 min) — Profile audit:**
- Open your LinkedIn profile. Read it like a stranger would.
- Your headline must do one of two things: state what you do + who you do it for, OR make a bold claim that creates curiosity. If it says "Co-Founder at [Company] | Building [thing]" — rewrite it.
  - Weak: *"Co-Founder @ Acme | B2B SaaS | Growth"*
  - Strong: *"Helping B2B SaaS teams build pipeline from LinkedIn without paid ads | Co-Founder @ Acme"*
- Your About section should open with the problem you solve, not your career history. Rewrite the first 2 sentences if they start with "I" or your company name.
- Add a Featured section with your best post, a case study link, or a newsletter signup. This is real estate most people waste.

**Afternoon (45 min) — POV definition:**
- Answer these three questions in a doc (no editing, just write):
  1. What do most people in my market believe that I think is wrong?
  2. What has my experience shown me that most people in my space haven't seen?
  3. What would I want a prospect to believe after following me for 90 days?
- Extract 2–3 single-sentence POV pillars from your answers. Write them down. These are your content anchors for the next 90 days.

**Output:** Improved profile. 2–3 written POV pillars.

---

### Day 2 — Content Audit + ICP Naming (60 min)

**Goal:** Know who you're writing for and what you've already got to work with.

**Step 1 (20 min) — Name your ICP reader:**
Write a 1-paragraph description of the one person you're writing for. Include:
- Their title and company size
- What they're responsible for that's keeping them up at night
- What they read, follow, or search for
- What they've tried that hasn't worked

This is your "reader brief." Keep it open in a tab while you write every post.

**Step 2 (40 min) — Content audit:**
- Go back through your last 20 LinkedIn posts (or your competitors' / peers' posts if you're starting fresh).
- Tag each as: TOFU (awareness), MOFU (consideration), or BOFU (decision-stage).
- Count the ratio. Most people find 80–90% is TOFU. Identify which posts got real engagement vs. just likes.
- Write down: What are the 3 topics where I have the most genuine depth?

**Output:** Named ICP profile. Tagged content audit. 3 topic areas to own.

---

### Day 3 — Build Your Content Bank (90 min)

**Goal:** Never stare at a blank page again. Build 3 weeks of post ideas in one sitting.

**Step 1 (30 min) — Talk to your sales team or review recent deals:**
- Ask for the top 3 objections prospects raise before signing.
- Ask what question came up in the last 5 discovery calls that the rep had to answer manually.
- Review 2–3 recent lost deals: why did they not buy?

These are content briefs. Write them down.

**Step 2 (60 min) — Build the idea bank:**
Create a simple table with 4 columns: Post idea | Post type | POV pillar it connects to | Format (text / carousel / story)

Populate it with a minimum of 21 ideas — enough for 3 weeks at 3 posts/week. Ideas can be rough. They don't need to be polished.

Idea sources:
- Your own POV pillars (each pillar = at least 5 post angles)
- Sales objections (each = 1 objection neutralizer post)
- ICP pain points (write from their world, not yours)
- Things you disagree with that are commonly accepted in your space
- A recent customer win or deal with a clear story arc

**Output:** 21+ post ideas organized and tagged. 3 objection neutralizer posts identified.

---

### Day 4 — Write Your First 3 Posts (90 min)

**Goal:** Get real content drafted and scheduled. Stop preparing. Start publishing.

Write 3 posts today using the Hook → Insight → Example → Takeaway framework from this playbook.

**Post 1 — POV Post (Monday):**
Take your strongest POV pillar. Write a post that states it directly, explains why most people get it wrong, and gives one piece of proof. Keep it to 150–200 words. End with a one-line conclusion, not a question.

**Post 2 — Tactical Post (Wednesday):**
Take one tactic you use internally that your ICP would find useful. Write it in step format if it has steps. Write it as a story if it has a narrative. Keep every point specific — replace every vague word with a concrete one.

**Post 3 — Story Post (Friday):**
Take one real situation: a deal, a mistake, a customer call, a product decision. Write it in chronological order. Situation → What happened → What you learned. Don't moralize at the end. Let the story do it.

**Formatting rules for all three posts:**
- Line breaks every 1–2 sentences. White space is readability.
- No bullet points in the first 3 lines.
- No hashtags in the body. Put 2–3 at the very end if you use them.
- Read each post out loud before scheduling.

**Output:** 3 posts written, proofed, and scheduled in LinkedIn's native scheduler (Mon/Wed/Fri of next week).

---

### Day 5 — Set Up Your Repurposing System (60 min)

**Goal:** Turn your content workflow from ad-hoc into a machine.

**Step 1 (20 min) — Create the repurposing tracker:**
Build a simple Notion or Google Sheet with columns:
- Original insight
- LinkedIn text post (link when published)
- Carousel version (yes / no / done)
- Video script (yes / no / done)
- Newsletter section (yes / no / done)

**Step 2 (40 min) — Take your best post from Day 4 and repurpose it now:**
- Turn Post 1 (POV post) into a 6-slide carousel outline: Slide 1 = hook, Slides 2–5 = one point per slide, Slide 6 = takeaway + CTA.
- Write a 90-second video script version of the same post. Format: 15 seconds of hook, 45 seconds of insight + example, 30 seconds of takeaway.

You don't need to record the video today. You need the script ready so the barrier to recording is zero.

**Output:** Repurposing tracker created. 1 post already planned into 3 formats.

---

### Day 6 — Sales Enablement Setup (45 min)

**Goal:** Make your content work in the sales funnel, not just the content funnel.

**Step 1 (15 min) — Brief your sales team:**
Send a Slack message or schedule a 15-minute sync. Tell them: "I'm going to tag posts each week that you can use in outreach. I'll tell you when to use each one."

**Step 2 (20 min) — Create the sales content doc:**
Build a simple Google Doc with sections organized by objection:
- "Prospect says onboarding is too complex" → [link to post]
- "Prospect questions ROI" → [link to post]
- "Prospect says they'll wait until next quarter" → [link to post]

You won't fill all of these today. Fill 2–3 with the posts you wrote on Day 4.

**Step 3 (10 min) — Define your weekly tagging ritual:**
Every Friday, after reviewing the week's posts, tag 1 post as "sales-ready" in the doc. This takes 5 minutes and keeps the system alive.

**Output:** Sales-ready content doc created. Posts tagged and briefed to the team.

---

### Day 7 — Review, Metrics Setup, and Week 2 Planning (60 min)

**Goal:** Build the habit loop that keeps this playbook running past week 1.

**Step 1 (20 min) — Metrics baseline:**
Set up your LinkedIn analytics baseline. Screenshot or log:
- Current follower count
- Average impressions per post (last 30 days, if available)
- Average engagement rate (reactions + comments / impressions)

These are your baseline numbers. You will compare against them in 30 days. Do not obsess over them weekly. Check them once a month.

**What to track instead, weekly:**
- Did I post 3x this week? (yes/no)
- Did I get at least 1 DM or comment from someone in my ICP?
- Did the sales team use any content in outreach this week?

**Step 2 (20 min) — Week 2 content planning:**
Pull 3 more ideas from your content bank (built Day 3). Assign them to Mon/Wed/Fri slots. Draft the hooks for each — just the first line. Schedule time to write the full posts on Monday morning.

**Step 3 (20 min) — Retrospective:**
Answer in writing:
- What felt hard to write this week, and why?
- Which post am I least confident about? What would make it sharper?
- What's one thing I learned about my audience from the comments or DMs this week?

These answers feed next week's content. This is how the system compounds.

**Output:** Metrics baseline logged. Week 2 planned. Retro written.

---

### What Weeks 2–4 Look Like

After the first week, the operating rhythm is:
- **Monday morning (30 min):** Write and schedule the week's 3 posts.
- **Friday afternoon (15 min):** Review engagement. Tag 1 sales-ready post. Add 3 new ideas to the bank. Write the retro.
- **Month-end (45 min):** Pull analytics. Compare to baseline. Identify the 1–2 posts that outperformed. Understand why. Do more of that.

You are not trying to go viral. You are trying to build a consistent presence that your ICP notices, trusts, and acts on over 90 days. That is the actual goal.

---

## Closing Thought

The highest-leverage move in LinkedIn for B2B SaaS is not a tactic — it's picking one person in your company who has genuine opinions, giving them 3 months to develop a voice, and measuring pipeline influenced (not follower count). Everything in this playbook serves that goal. Most companies skip to tactics and wonder why nothing compounds.

Build the voice first. The distribution follows.
