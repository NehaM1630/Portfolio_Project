## Video 1: "The Dark Social Framework for B2B SaaS"

**Published:** February 2026
**Duration:** 52 minutes

---

### [05:00] — What Dark Social Actually Is

"Dark social is every conversation, every share, every mention that happens about your brand that you will never see in your CRM. The Slack message where a CMO says to their team — 'I've been following this guy for six months, I think we should talk to him.' The group chat where a VP says — 'did you see what Walker posted this morning?' That's dark social. It's real influence that shows up as direct traffic or zero in your attribution model."

"B2B attribution tools are designed to measure demand capture — the last touchpoint before someone fills out a form. They are completely blind to demand creation. And demand creation is where 80% of the actual influence happens."

---

### [14:20] — Why This Changes Everything for LinkedIn Content

"If you accept that most B2B buying influence is dark social — things that happen privately that you can't measure — then your entire relationship to LinkedIn content has to change. You stop optimizing for clicks. You stop optimizing for impressions. You start optimizing for one thing: is this post worth forwarding to a colleague?"

"When a CMO reads your post and thinks 'my whole team needs to see this' and pastes it into Slack — that is the highest value action that can happen from a LinkedIn post. It will show up nowhere in your analytics. But it moves deals."

"We had clients come to us who said they found us from a Google search. We'd dig in and find out — someone on their team had been following our content for eight months. The Google search was the last step of a journey that started on LinkedIn. They didn't even know to attribute it to LinkedIn. Neither did their CRM."

---

### [22:40] — The Weekly Content System

"Here's exactly how I do it. Monday, I identify the one piece of conventional wisdom in demand gen or GTM that I most disagree with right now. I try to find something that CMOs at SaaS companies are doing or believing that I think is wrong and I can prove it with data."

"Tuesday, I write the post. One contrarian claim, three pieces of supporting evidence, one implication for what they should do instead. Never more than 600 words."

"Wednesday, I post it. Then I watch the comments for the next 24 hours. I'm not looking for likes. I'm looking for people who respond with a challenge, a question, or their own experience. Those are the people who are actually engaged with the problem I'm solving."

"Thursday, I go through those comments and I identify the three to five people who engaged most thoughtfully. Not the ones who said 'great post' — the ones who pushed back or shared a real situation they're facing."

"Friday, I send each of them a personal DM. Not a pitch. Something like — 'Your comment about X really stood out. I've actually built a diagnostic for exactly that situation — would it be useful if I shared it?' That's how LinkedIn becomes a sales motion. The comment section is where you find the people. The DM is where the conversation starts."

---

### [35:00] — Scaling Beyond One Founder's Voice

"When you're at zero to ten million ARR, the founder's LinkedIn presence is your entire demand creation engine. That's the lever. The founder posting three times a week about what they're learning, what they believe, what they disagree with — that is the most efficient marketing spend you can make at that stage. It costs you time, not money."

"But at ten million ARR and above, the founder alone can't carry it. You need what I call an executive content program. Get three to five people in senior roles posting regularly. Each one reaches a slightly different sub-audience of your ICP. The combined reach of five authentic personal voices at three posts per week each dwarfs what any company LinkedIn page will ever generate."

"At Refine Labs, we ran this experiment with our own team. Five senior people each posting three times a week. We tracked every piece of pipeline for a year. That employee content program generated more pipeline than all of our paid LinkedIn ads combined. More pipeline. From zero ad spend. Just people sharing what they genuinely knew and believed."

## Video 2: "Why Most B2B LinkedIn Strategies Fail"

**Published:** November 2025
**Duration:** 31 minutes

---

### [03:20] — The #1 LinkedIn Mistake

"The number one mistake I see from B2B SaaS companies on LinkedIn is this: they're writing for the algorithm instead of for their buyer. They're trying to get likes from a broad audience because someone told them impressions equal brand awareness equals pipeline. That is not how B2B buying works."

"You don't need a thousand likes. You need three CMOs to read your post and think — 'I need to have a conversation with this person.' Designing content for ICP engagement versus designing for general engagement are fundamentally different content strategies. Most companies don't know which one they're doing."

---

### [11:00] — What "ICP-First" Content Actually Looks Like

"ICP-first means before you write anything, you have already answered four questions. One — who exactly is reading this? Not 'B2B marketers.' A CMO at a Series B SaaS company between twenty and a hundred employees who is trying to prove marketing ROI to a skeptical CFO. That specific."

"Two — what do they currently believe that I'm going to challenge or confirm? Three — what do I want them to think, feel, or do after reading this? Four — why is my perspective on this credible? What have I done or seen that gives me the right to say this?"

"If you can't answer all four, you're writing for yourself. And writing for yourself is the fastest way to have a large, unengaged, irrelevant audience."

---

### [18:30] — What Walker Actually Tracks

"People always ask me what metrics matter for LinkedIn. Here's my actual measurement stack. First: qualified DMs per week. How many people I've never talked to before reached out because of something I posted. Second: pipeline opportunities sourced from LinkedIn per quarter — deals where LinkedIn content is in the origin story. Third: brand mentions in discovery calls. How often prospects say 'I've been following you for...' at the start of a call."

"Likes, followers, impressions — I look at them occasionally to make sure nothing is catastrophically wrong. But they are not my metrics. They are vanity proxies for a strategy that might not be working at all. I've had posts with ten thousand impressions that started zero conversations. I've had posts with eight hundred impressions that started three qualified conversations that turned into clients."

"Optimize for conversations. Everything else is noise."
